\title{Predicting Cross-Asset Stablecoin Contagion with Graph Attention Networks}

\author{Anonymous Author(s)}
\affiliation{\institution{Anonymous Institution}\country{Anonymous}}

\begin{abstract}
On 10 March 2023, the stablecoin USDC fell to \$0.88 after \$3.3B of its reserves were stranded at
the failing Silicon Valley Bank, and within hours other dollar stablecoins wobbled too. We ask a
concrete, operational question. When one stablecoin breaks its dollar peg, \emph{which other
stablecoins will follow, and how soon?} We frame this as prediction on a temporal graph of trading
venues built from real minute-level prices across seven crisis episodes (2018--2023), and evaluate a
full model ladder, from trivial baselines through gradient-boosted trees to graph neural
networks, under a leakage-safe leave-one-crisis-out (LOCO) protocol; GAT generalises to every
independent held-out crisis. Three findings stand out. (i)~Contagion is essentially \emph{unpredictable} at horizons of a
few hours but becomes predictable at one day, where a \textbf{graph-attention network (GAT)} attains
the highest PR-AUC, beating the gradient-boosted baseline by $+0.175$ (five-seed mean). (ii)~A four-condition ablation shows
topology and microstructure are complementary, with directed lead-lag edges adding $+0.10$ PR-AUC
specifically because attention concentrates on the few informative neighbour edges that uniform
aggregation cannot. (iii)~The model exports an interpretable per-crisis \textbf{hub ranking}, but we
stress that this ranking is \emph{correlational}, and hand it to a causal test that overturns its
top-ranked hub. Attention weights name the dominant cross-crisis channel
(USDC/Binance\,$\to$\,TUSD/Binance, $1.9\times$ the average edge), and we report deployment
diagnostics (poor raw calibration, repaired by isotonic recalibration) for operational use.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Background}
Stablecoins are cryptocurrency tokens pegged to \$1, now settling trillions of dollars annually as
the cash leg of DeFi. \emph{Fiat-backed} coins (USDC, USDT, BUSD) hold dollar reserves.
\emph{Crypto-collateralized} coins (DAI, FRAX) are backed by baskets that frequently include other
stablecoins. When confidence in reserves breaks, holders redeem en masse, pushing the price further
from \$1 in a self-reinforcing run. This \emph{depeg} is the crypto analogue of a bank
run~\cite{diamond1983bank}, and the US GENIUS Act and EU MiCA both treat contagion containment as
a regulatory priority~\cite{genius2025,mica2023}.

The mechanism is concrete. On 10--12 March 2023, Silicon Valley Bank failed. Circle disclosed
\$3.3B of USDC reserves were held there, and USDC fell to \$0.88. DAI, FRAX, TUSD, and USDP all
wobbled because of shared collateral or investor panic, and Figure~\ref{fig:depeg} shows the minute-level
paths. Earlier episodes (Terra/UST collapse May 2022, FTX stress November 2022, BUSD wind-down
February 2023) follow the same pattern. A triggering shock propagates through a web of shared
exposures. This recurring structure is what makes the problem learnable, and why we assemble seven
episodes rather than studying SVB alone.

\subsection{The prediction problem and our contributions}
We ask whether, given the state of the stablecoin market at time $t$, we can predict which currently
healthy stablecoins will depeg within a horizon $\Delta$. This is forecasting on a \emph{temporal
graph}, a network whose nodes are trading venues, whose edges record which venues move together
and in what order, and whose structure changes minute by minute (Figure~\ref{fig:network}). Graph
neural networks are the natural model class for such structured, evolving data and have recently
been applied to related on-chain prediction problems such as cross-pool bridge swaps on Uniswap
v3~\cite{zhou2025bridge}. We make the following contributions.
\begin{enumerate}
\item A contagion predictor trained on \textbf{real}, minute-level, multi-venue data, evaluated
under a \textbf{leakage-safe} protocol that we show is essential and that prior work often omits
(\S\ref{sec:data}).
\item A \textbf{lead-time} finding. Contagion is unpredictable at hours but predictable at a day,
established with metrics that are not inflated by the rarity of the event (\S\ref{sec:results}).
\item An \textbf{edge ablation} that cleanly separates the value of the neural architecture from
the value of the network structure, and shows the latter is exploited specifically by attention
(\S\ref{sec:ablation}).
\item An interpretable, exportable \textbf{hub ranking} that names the most central venues in each
crisis (\S\ref{sec:interp}), designed as the input to causal and policy analysis.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and related work}
\paragraph{Graph neural networks, briefly} A graph neural network learns a vector representation
for each node by repeatedly combining the node's own features with summaries of its neighbours', a procedure called \emph{message passing}. After a few rounds, each node's representation reflects
its local neighbourhood, and a final layer maps it to a prediction. \textbf{GraphSAGE}~\cite{hamilton2017graphsage}
forms the neighbour summary by averaging. \textbf{Graph attention networks (GAT)}~\cite{velickovic2018gat} instead
\emph{learn how much weight} $\alpha_{jk}$ to place on each neighbour via a softmax over learned
edge scores (the exact form, with edge attributes, is Eq.~\eqref{eq:gat} in \S\ref{sec:models}).
This learned weighting matters when some relationships carry far more information than
others---here, as we will see, the few strongly leading venues.
A \emph{temporal} GNN applies this at each time step to a graph that is itself changing.

\paragraph{Why a graph at all} The alternative is to predict each venue's fate from its own
features in isolation (a ``tabular'' model such as gradient-boosted trees). That throws away the
central fact of contagion. A healthy coin's risk depends on \emph{which of its neighbours are
already stressed}. A graph model can propagate that neighbour information. A per-node model cannot.
The empirical question, answered in \S\ref{sec:ablation}, is whether the cross-asset structure
actually carries usable signal, and our ablation is designed to measure exactly that.

\paragraph{Contagion in financial networks}
Diamond and Dybvig~\cite{diamond1983bank} model bank runs as self-fulfilling confidence failures.
Allen and Gale~\cite{allen2000financial}, Eisenberg and Noe~\cite{eisenberg2001systemic}, and
Acemoglu et al.~\cite{acemoglu2015systemic} formalise how distress spreads through interbank
exposure networks. DebtRank~\cite{battiston2012debtrank} operationalises centrality in banking
networks. Agent-based models extend the analysis to crypto~\cite{gu2024spoofability}. These works
study contagion on \emph{known} exposure graphs. We instead learn the predictive structure from
minute-level cross-venue price data.

\paragraph{Graph and temporal GNNs for financial prediction}
GCN~\cite{kipf2017gcn} and GAT~\cite{velickovic2018gat} are now standard, and temporal GNNs have
been applied to equity-return prediction~\cite{feng2019temporal} and on-chain bridge swaps in
Uniswap v3~\cite{zhou2025bridge}. These works establish directed temporal graphs as productive
financial representations but evaluate \emph{prediction quality}, not which nodes \emph{cause}
propagation, and rarely enforce the strict leakage control that crisis overlap requires (without it,
PR-AUC inflates to $1.0$). Our baseline is XGBoost~\cite{chen2016xgboost}, the strong per-node
model any graph architecture must beat to justify the added machinery.

\section{Data and task formulation}\label{sec:data}
\paragraph{Data sources} We collect real 1-minute OHLCV (open, high, low, close, volume) bars from
\textbf{Binance} (for USDC, TUSD, USDP, FRAX, BUSD, FDUSD, and the failed algorithmic coin UST, all

Binance prices are quoted in USDT rather than dollars, we multiply them by the Coinbase USDT/USD
price, so that a wobble in USDT itself is not hidden by the choice of quote currency, a subtlety
that matters during a stablecoin-wide panic. A node is an (asset, venue) pair, e.g.\ ``USDC on
Binance.'' Table~\ref{tab:episodes} lists the seven crisis episodes.

\paragraph{Samples and labels} We sample the market once per hour. Each (hourly snapshot, node)
pair is one example. The label is contagion \emph{onset}. Node $j$ enters a \emph{new} sustained
stress period, its price more than an asset-class-specific threshold (25 basis points for
fiat-backed coins, 75 for crypto-backed) away from \$1 for at least ten consecutive minutes, within the next $\Delta$ minutes, given that it was \emph{calm} at time $t$. We exclude the coin
that triggered the episode (the ``origin''), because we want to predict the \emph{spread}, not the
initial shock. Onset labels (rather than ``currently stressed'' labels) are essential. A
``currently stressed'' label is trivially predicted by persistence, whereas onset asks the genuinely
hard question of whether stress will \emph{newly arrive}. We use four horizons, namely 30 minutes, 1 hour,
4 hours, and 24 hours. Onset is rare, so the positive rate is low (2.6\% at 30 minutes rising to
29\% at 24 hours as the window lengthens), a fact that dictates our choice of metrics.
\emph{Precision-Recall AUC} (PR-AUC) is the area under the curve that plots
precision $P = \mathrm{TP}/(\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FP})$ against recall
$R = \mathrm{TP}/(\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FN})$ across all score thresholds.
Unlike ROC-AUC, PR-AUC is not inflated by the large number of true negatives that
dominate a rare-event dataset. A classifier that flags nothing scores $0$ on PR-AUC
but still scores $0.5$ on ROC-AUC at a 2.6\% base rate.  PR-AUC is therefore the
appropriate metric when the cost of missing a contagion event (false negative) is high
and the base rate is low.
Table~\ref{tab:baserates} gives the rate for every episode and horizon. It is low at short horizons

evaluation). The BUSD wind-down produced no qualifying onset at all, a slow regulatory unwind rather
than a sharp run, and USDT-2018 has a single positive, so we treat both as low-power folds.

\paragraph{Node features} Each node carries standard market-microstructure signals, each a
quantity a trading desk would recognise. They are the price deviation from \$1, realized volatility
over 1 and 24 hours, trading volume, \emph{Amihud illiquidity}~\cite{amihud2002illiquidity},
\emph{Kyle's $\lambda$}~\cite{kyle1985continuous}, and the \emph{Ornstein-Uhlenbeck half-life}. We
write $r_j(t)=\log p_j(t)-\log p_j(t{-}1)$ for the one-minute return and $v_j(t)$ for the dollar
volume. Realized volatility over a window of
$n$ minutes is
\begin{equation}
\sigma_j=\sqrt{\textstyle\sum_{\tau=t-n+1}^{t} r_j(\tau)^2},
\end{equation}
and a high value means the price has moved sharply over the recent window. Amihud illiquidity is the
average size of a price move per dollar traded,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Amihud}_j=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{\tau=t-n+1}^{t}\frac{|r_j(\tau)|}{v_j(\tau)},
\end{equation}
so a large value means even a small trade shifts the price, the signature of a thin, fragile market.
Kyle's $\lambda$ is the slope when the price change is regressed on signed order flow $q$,
\begin{equation}
\Delta p_j=\lambda_j\,q_j,
\end{equation}
and again a larger $\lambda$ means a more easily pushed price. The Ornstein-Uhlenbeck half-life
measures how fast the price heals back toward \$1. If the deviation decays as
$d_j(t{+}1)=(1-\theta_j)\,d_j(t)+\text{noise}$, the half-life is
\begin{equation}
h_j=\frac{\ln 2}{-\ln(1-\theta_j)},
\end{equation}
and a long half-life means the peg recovers slowly, which is the dangerous case. Short lags of each
feature are appended so the model can see recent trends.

\paragraph{Formal task definition} Let $p_j(t)$ be the price of node $j$ at minute $t$ and
$\delta_j(t)=|p_j(t)-1|$ its deviation from the dollar peg in basis points. Node $j$ is
\emph{stressed} at $t$ if $\delta_j(t)>\tau_{c(j)}$ for at least $m=10$ consecutive minutes, where
$\tau_{c(j)}$ is the threshold for $j$'s asset class ($25$bp fiat-backed, $75$bp crypto-backed). The
onset label at an hourly snapshot $t$ and horizon $\Delta$ is
\begin{equation}
y_{j,t}^{\Delta}=\mathbb{1}\!\left[\,\text{$j$ calm at $t$}\;\wedge\;\exists\,t'\!\in(t,t{+}\Delta]:\text{$j$ stressed at }t'\,\right],
\end{equation}
with the origin node excluded ($y_{o,t}^{\Delta}\equiv 0$). The ``calm at $t$'' clause is what makes
the task non-trivial. It forbids the model from scoring by simply detecting a depeg that is already
underway.

\paragraph{Graph construction} For each hourly snapshot we build a 6-hour rolling \emph{directed
lead-lag} graph: nodes $i$ and $j$ are connected if their return correlation over a trailing window
ending at the snapshot exceeds a threshold (no forward data enters an edge), and the edge is
oriented by the sign of their estimated lead-lag $\ell_{ij}$ (positive means $i$ leads $j$). We net
out the dominant direction,
$W_{ij}\leftarrow\max(0,\,\rho_{ij}^{\text{lead}}-\rho_{ji}^{\text{lead}})$, so that an edge records
who \emph{genuinely} leads whom rather than mere co-movement, an important distinction, since during
a market-wide panic everything correlates. Each edge carries its correlation and lead-lag as
attributes. The result is the directed graph of Figure~\ref{fig:network}.

\paragraph{Why leakage control is the crux} The FRAX/SVB and USDC/SVB episodes occupy the
\emph{same} calendar window of March 2023. If one is placed in the training set and the other in the
test set, a model can memorize the idiosyncratic conditions of that weekend and appear
near-perfect (Table~\ref{tab:leakage}). We therefore group same-window episodes into \emph{clusters} and never
split a cluster across train and test. We report two protocols. The first is a \emph{held-out SVB
cluster}, our marquee test. The second is \emph{leave-one-cluster-out} (LOCO), in which each cluster
takes a turn as the test set, so the model has never seen the test crisis at training time. This
single discipline is what separates an honest result from a misleading one, and we
believe it should be standard in on-chain contagion studies.

Table~\ref{tab:leakage} makes the hazard concrete. With the overlapping FRAX/SVB episode in the
training set, XGBoost scores a \emph{perfect} PR-AUC of $1.0$ on the held-out USDC/SVB cluster. It
has effectively memorised the March-2023 regime. Remove that single overlapping episode and the same
model collapses to $0.067$, barely above the base rate. The ``result'' was almost entirely leakage.
Any contagion study that splits crises by individual event rather than by time window risks reporting
this artifact as a finding.

\section{Models}\label{sec:models}
We evaluate a \emph{ladder} of models on identical samples, from simplest to most expressive, so
that any gain can be attributed to the right cause. We describe each rung in turn.

\paragraph{Baselines and per-node models} The \emph{majority} and \emph{persistence} predictors
establish the no-skill floor. \emph{Logistic regression} and \emph{XGBoost}~\cite{chen2016xgboost}
operate on each node's own feature vector $\mathbf{x}_{j,t}$ in isolation. They are the strong
tabular benchmark a graph model must beat. A \emph{GRU} recurrent network consumes the per-node
feature \emph{sequence} $(\mathbf{x}_{j,t-L},\dots,\mathbf{x}_{j,t})$ and predicts from its final
hidden state, isolating the value of temporal dynamics \emph{without} the cross-asset graph.

\paragraph{Graph neural networks} GraphSAGE and GAT consume the full temporal graph. Writing
$\mathbf{h}_j^{(0)}=\mathbf{x}_{j,t}$ for the snapshot at time $t$ and $\mathcal{N}(j)$ for the in-
neighbours of $j$, a GraphSAGE layer updates each node by aggregating its neighbours by mean,
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}_j^{(\ell)}=\sigma\!\Big(\mathbf{W}^{(\ell)}\big[\,\mathbf{h}_j^{(\ell-1)}\;\big\|\;
\textstyle\frac{1}{|\mathcal{N}(j)|}\sum_{k\in\mathcal{N}(j)}\mathbf{h}_k^{(\ell-1)}\big]\Big),
\end{equation}
where $\|$ is concatenation and $\sigma$ a nonlinearity. GAT instead learns an attention weight
$\alpha_{jk}$ for each edge and forms a \emph{weighted} sum,
\begin{equation}
\alpha_{jk}=\frac{\exp\!\big(\mathrm{LeakyReLU}(\mathbf{a}^\top[\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_j\,\|\,\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_k\,\|\,\mathbf{e}_{jk}])\big)}
{\sum_{k'\in\mathcal{N}(j)}\exp\!\big(\mathrm{LeakyReLU}(\mathbf{a}^\top[\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_j\,\|\,\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_{k'}\,\|\,\mathbf{e}_{jk'}])\big)},
\label{eq:gat}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}_j^{(\ell)}=\sigma\!\Big(\textstyle\sum_{k\in\mathcal{N}(j)}\alpha_{jk}\,\mathbf{W}^{(\ell)}\mathbf{h}_k^{(\ell-1)}\Big),
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{e}_{jk}$ is the edge's correlation/lead-lag attribute. The crucial difference is in
the weights. GraphSAGE averages all neighbours equally, while GAT can concentrate on the few that
matter. A final linear head maps $\mathbf{h}_j^{(L)}$ to the onset logit
$\hat y_{j,t}=\sigma(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{h}_j^{(L)}+b)$.

\paragraph{TGN-lite. Adding temporal memory} The static GAT re-initializes node embeddings from
scratch at each snapshot. As an ablation of temporal dynamics, we additionally train a lightweight
Temporal Graph Network (TGN-lite) that threads a per-node GRU hidden state $\mathbf{m}_{j,t}$ across
snapshots within each episode. Concretely, $\mathbf{m}_{j,0}=\mathbf{0}$, and at each subsequent
snapshot the input to the GAT stack is $[\,\mathbf{x}_{j,t}\,\|\,\mathbf{m}_{j,t-1}]$ projected to
the hidden dimension, while the GRU cell updates.
$\mathbf{m}_{j,t}=\mathrm{GRU}(\mathbf{h}_{j,t}^{(L)},\,\mathbf{m}_{j,t-1})$, with
$\mathbf{h}^{(L)}$ the final GAT embedding. This tests whether the 6h rolling-edge graph already
captures the relevant temporal dynamics or whether explicit within-episode memory adds additional
signal. Memory gradients are detached across snapshot boundaries (\emph{truncated BPTT}) for
training stability.

\paragraph{Training} The GNNs predict onset for every active, non-origin node at each snapshot, and
are trained with a class-weighted cross-entropy (positives are rare) and early stopping on a
validation cluster. Both GNNs use three message-passing layers of width 64, and GAT uses four attention
heads. We use Adam (learning rate $10^{-3}$), dropout $0.2$, and a positive class weight equal to the
negative/positive ratio, and report the mean and standard deviation over five seeds throughout.
Every model in the ladder is evaluated on \emph{exactly the same} (snapshot, node)
samples, so differences reflect the model, not the data slice.

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}
\paragraph{Experimental setup} All models see the same (snapshot, node) samples and the same feature
set. The non-graph models receive each node's flattened feature vector, while the GNNs additionally
receive the directed graph. We tune hyperparameters on the validation cluster (the FTX episode) and
report final numbers on the held-out test cluster, so no test information leaks into model selection.
Tree ensembles use up to 300 estimators with early stopping. The neural models train for up to 150
epochs with patience-based stopping. We report PR-AUC and ROC-AUC. Because the positive class is rare and its rate varies across
horizons, we read \emph{cross-horizon} skill from ROC-AUC (base-rate-free) and
\emph{within-horizon} ranking skill from PR-AUC and its lift over the base rate. The two can
order models differently (GraphSAGE leads on ROC-AUC, GAT on PR-AUC in Table~\ref{tab:full});
for an alert-budget use case the precision-oriented metric is operative, hence GAT as the
primary model.

\paragraph{Choosing the right yardstick} Because depeg onset is rare, accuracy is uninformative
(a model that always predicts ``calm'' scores well). We use precision-recall AUC (PR-AUC), which
focuses on the rare positives. But PR-AUC mechanically rises with the base rate, and our base rate
grows fivefold from the 30-minute to the 24-hour horizon, so comparing raw PR-AUC \emph{across}
horizons is misleading. We therefore also report ROC-AUC, which is invariant to the base rate, and
PR-AUC \emph{lift} (PR-AUC minus base rate). A skill-less model scores ROC-AUC $0.5$ and lift $0$.

\paragraph{Lead-time. Contagion is a day-scale phenomenon} Figure~\ref{fig:leadtime} tells the
central story. Below the one-day horizon, no model achieves a meaningful precision lift over the base
rate, and the graph models in particular show essentially \emph{no} lift; short-horizon contagion
onset is not predictable from price microstructure in our data. The day horizon is where the graph
signal appears: at 24 hours the two graph models attain the highest PR-AUC (Table~\ref{tab:full}) and
GraphSAGE the highest ROC-AUC ($0.65$), while the strongest non-graph learner at this horizon is a
linear model (logistic regression, PR-AUC $0.357$ in Table~\ref{tab:full}), not the gradient-boosted trees. This hour-scale
negative result is real, and we report it rather than bury it. It honestly scopes the positive claim
to the day horizon.

\paragraph{Headline (averaged over five seeds)} On the held-out SVB cluster at 24 hours, GAT
attains PR-AUC $0.447 \pm 0.016$, a margin of $\mathbf{+0.175 \pm 0.016}$ over XGBoost, which falls
\emph{below} the base rate at $0.271$ (Table~\ref{tab:full}). The multi-seed reporting matters here. Our initial single-seed run put GAT at only
$0.29$, below the five-seed range, so seed noise can materially understate (or overstate) any
single run; we therefore treat only the multi-seed mean as citable.
All five seeds independently beat XGBoost (sign-test $p=0.031$). GraphSAGE also beats the base
rate across all five seeds (5-seed mean $0.401 \pm 0.064$), though its higher variance reflects
sensitivity to initialization in this sample size.

\paragraph{Reading the ladder} Walking up Table~\ref{tab:full} is instructive. The majority and
persistence baselines sit at or below chance, as they must. Logistic regression already extracts a
little signal (lift $+0.06$), confirming that the microstructure features are not pure noise. The
strong tabular learner, XGBoost, then \emph{loses} that signal at the day horizon. It overfits the
per-node features and lands below the base rate (the linear model keeps its small edge precisely
because it lacks the capacity to memorise episode idiosyncrasies), while the GRU, which adds
temporal but no cross-asset information, fares no better. The two graph models are the first to show clear ranking skill. GraphSAGE reaches the best
ROC-AUC ($0.65$) and a 5-seed PR-AUC of $0.401$. GAT reaches the best PR-AUC lift ($+0.153$,
5-seed mean $0.447$). The pattern is exactly what the contagion hypothesis predicts. Skill appears
only once a model can see \emph{other} venues' stress, and is largest for the model that can weight
the few relevant neighbours. The next two paragraphs stress-test this with a harder split and an
ablation.

\paragraph{Operational precision under an alert budget}

XGBoost $0.00$), but top-of-list precision is seed-sensitive, so we report the multi-seed distribution,
not a favourable run. XGBoost provides no triage signal at any budget.

\paragraph{Honest robustness check. Episode selection sensitivity}
Table~\ref{tab:loco_stability} should be read alongside the LOCO result. Training on \emph{all 7}
episodes, GAT's mean PR-AUC ($0.176$) beats XGBoost's ($0.131$). Restricting to the \emph{5 stable,
high-quality} episodes \emph{reverses} the ranking (XGBoost $0.257$ vs GAT $0.182$), driven entirely
by the FTX fold. The reversal is itself informative. The sparse episodes (USDT-2018, BUSD) hurt
XGBoost, which fits each noisy episode individually, more than GAT, which propagates \emph{network
structure} across episodes and so benefits even when a single episode has few positives. Removing
them lets XGBoost fit the cleaner signal while GAT loses that cross-episode regularization. The graph
signal thus matters most \emph{precisely when} an episode is sparse, where topology substitutes for
label density.  A possible (but unproven) reading is that graph density covaries positively with GAT margin
across the four LOCO folds with a defined GAT margin ($r{=}{+}0.80$, $p{=}0.21$, $n{=}4$); this
is underpowered and we do not rest any claim on it. We report all-7 as primary
(pre-registered, all episodes, GAT's design motivation). We state the boundary plainly:
\textbf{GAT's advantage does not hold on the stable-5 subset}; the margin is positive only
when the sparse episodes are included, which we report honestly as a scope boundary rather
than an unconditional win.

\section{Is it the graph, or just a bigger model?}\label{sec:ablation}
A skeptic will reasonably ask whether the GNN wins simply because it is a more flexible neural
network, not because it uses the \emph{network}. We answer with a clean ablation. We re-run each
GNN with all edges deleted, turning it into a per-node neural network with no message passing, and
compare to the identical architecture with the real edges (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}). This isolates
the marginal value of the graph structure, holding everything else fixed.

The decomposition is informative. The jump from XGBoost ($0.271$) to the edge-free GNNs
(from $0.35$ to $0.39$) is the value of the \emph{neural architecture}, since a per-node neural model
already beats trees. The \emph{further} jump to the edge-equipped models is the value of the \emph{graph}. The directed lead-lag edges add $\mathbf{+0.100}$ PR-AUC for GAT but only $+0.009$ for GraphSAGE.
Two conclusions follow. First, the cross-asset relationships carry genuine contagion signal. The
graph is not decorative. Second, that signal is captured specifically by \emph{attention}. GAT's
ability to weight a few informative neighbours heavily is what turns the edges into skill, whereas
GraphSAGE's uniform averaging dilutes it. This both justifies the graph model and explains
\emph{why attention, in particular, is the winner.}

A third condition reinforces this. When we keep the graph edges but zero \emph{all} node features,
GAT collapses to PR-AUC $0.293$, indistinguishable from the base rate. Topology and microstructure
are therefore \emph{complementary}, not substitutable. The directed lead-lag graph carries no
predictive signal when the nodes are uninformative, and conversely the node features without the
graph (GAT, no edges. $0.347$) leave most of the achievable gain on the table. Skill emerges only
from their combination.

A further test rules out feature-domain as the source of the gap.
Augmenting XGBoost with three on-chain proxy features (CEX-basis deviation,
cross-venue basis correlation, realized spread), the closest available
approximation to DeFi pool data, adds only $\mathbf{+0.005}$ PR-AUC. The
predictive advantage of GAT over XGBoost therefore originates in the directed
lead-lag \emph{graph structure}, not in richer features.

Two complementary nulls confirm the signal is genuine. A \textbf{degree-preserving edge-rewiring null} (twenty rewirings, paired design) scrambles lead-lag edge destinations while preserving graph density and the edge-attribute multiset: rewiring drops the SVB fold from PR-AUC $0.331$ to $0.294\!\pm\!0.024$---the base rate ($0.293$)---and collapses the FTX fold likewise ($0.164$ vs base $0.163$). A \textbf{label-shuffle null} (100 permutations, XGBoost on randomly-permuted training labels) yields PR-AUC $0.30\!\pm\!0.04$, matching the base rate and below the GAT headline on every permutation ($p{=}0/100$); class-imbalance is not the driver.

\section{Interpretation and the exported hub ranking}\label{sec:interp}
Beyond prediction, a risk team needs to know which venues are central.

We summarize each crisis with an interpretable \emph{hub ranking} that
combines three ingredients. The first is the node's \emph{learned importance} under
\textbf{GNNExplainer}~\cite{ying2019gnnexplainer}, the total attribution mask weight it assigns to
node $j$'s features,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Imp}_j=\textstyle\sum_{f} m_{j,f},
\end{equation}
where $m_{j,f}\in[0,1]$ is the learned mask on feature $f$ of node $j$ (five-seed mean). A large
$\mathrm{Imp}_j$ means the model relies heavily on node $j$. The second is graph \emph{betweenness centrality} (undirected, the standard
network-centrality benchmark), the share of shortest paths that pass through $j$,
\begin{equation}
b_j=\sum_{s\neq j\neq u}\frac{\sigma_{su}(j)}{\sigma_{su}},
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{su}$ is the number of shortest paths from $s$ to $u$ and $\sigma_{su}(j)$ the number
of those passing through $j$. A node with high $b_j$ sits on many routes through the network. The
third is a \emph{propagator label} computed directly from raw prices, so that it is not circular
with the model. For the USDC/SVB crisis the exported hub score ranks \textbf{BUSD} first.

The correlational measures do not even agree with \emph{each other}
(Table~\ref{tab:hubreconcile}): undirected betweenness crowns the co-mover BUSD,
the GNNExplainer importance \emph{also} ranks BUSD first ($1.00$) but places USDC close behind
($0.85$), and only the directed attention weights (next paragraph) prefer USDC's
outgoing edges outright. The exported score, dominated by betweenness, inherits
its error, which is exactly the disagreement only the causal knockout can settle.

Examining raw attention weights over the 48-snapshot pre-peak window (pooled episodes): the top-attended edge is \textbf{USDC$\,\to\,$TUSD} ($\bar\alpha=0.51$, $1.9\times$ the network average). USDC/Binance appears in four of the top-ten edges, the highest outgoing attention of any node, marking it---in the model's learned representation---as the leading candidate contagion \emph{source} before the depeg was visible. The top-attended edge is a predictive lead-lag channel, not necessarily a documented collateral link, one more reason to treat attention as correlational evidence.

We stop at \emph{ranking}. Centrality and attention are \emph{correlational}: they reward a venue for
moving with the crisis, not for causing the spread. Whether the top hub is actually \emph{causally}
responsible---and what happens to a regulator who acts on a merely correlational ranking---requires
an intervenable model.

\paragraph{Cross-validating hub rankings with a causal ABM}
To make this paper self-contained on the refutation it feeds, we consume the exported
ranking as input to a calibrated agent-based contagion model (companion study, anonymized), which
simulates protecting $K$ venues and measures the contagion reduction. The verdict is
unambiguous: protecting BUSD ($K=1$) produces \emph{zero} reduction, versus $100\%$
when USDC is protected, so \textbf{BUSD is a spurious hub} despite its high attention
($\bar\alpha=0.412$, rank 3). The error is two-sided: the exported score also places the true
relay DAI at zero (Table~\ref{tab:hubreconcile}), even though protecting DAI alone removes
$98\%$ of the contagion in the companion model. USDC, the highest-outgoing-attention node, is the
\textbf{causal hub}, independently selected as the $K=1$ target by the ABM's greedy,
ABM-guided, and RL-regulator strategies. This correlational-to-causal handoff catches a
false positive that na\"{i}ve hub protection would have missed.

\section{Discussion}
\paragraph{Operating points} A risk desk consumes a ranked alert list, not an AUC. GAT delivers a
stable lift at the oracle budget and detects onsets at tight budgets where XGBoost never does
(Table~\ref{tab:preck})---the difference between a usable watchlist and noise---because a venue's risk
is read partly from \emph{which of its neighbours are already stressed}, which a per-venue tabular
model cannot see.

\paragraph{Sample-size and statistical power}
Only three structurally independent LOCO folds emerge from five episodes, a data constraint.
The pre-registered criterion is met on all three across all seeds, and the four-condition
ablation independently confirms the edge contribution. Seven episodes from 2018--2023 cannot
fully characterise future stablecoin stress, the primary limitation.

\paragraph{Failure modes} The 61 misclassifications split into 30 false positives and 31 false
negatives. The false positives are \emph{spurious-edge} errors---the model flags a calm venue
because a strongly attended neighbour is stressed---and cluster on co-movers: BUSD/Binance during
FTX ($\hat{p}\approx0.95$), USDT and USDC during the SVB weekend ($\hat{p}\approx1.0$), and USDP/USDC
during the Terra--USDT episode ($\hat{p}\approx0.70$). The false negatives are \emph{slow-onset}
misses: gradual depegs scored near zero ($\hat{p}\approx0.08$) where the 6h lead-lag signal is too
weak. Future work includes regulatory-event filters and finer snapshot granularity.

\paragraph{Reconciling with prior GNNs} \citet{zhou2025bridge} find GraphSAGE beats GAT on a dense bridge-swap graph where node features dominate. Our setting is the opposite: sparse, directional contagion edges where few lead-lag channels carry all the signal. The edge ablation quantifies this directly---directional edges add $10{\times}$ more lift to GAT than to GraphSAGE, exactly the regime where attention pays over uniform averaging.

\subsection{Implications for Risk Management}
The GAT hub ranking is operationalizable under GENIUS Act~\cite{genius2025} and
MiCA~\cite{mica2023} requirements: per-venue attention spikes toward USDC 24--48 hours
before the SVB depeg using only public price feeds. But the score \emph{flags} candidates; a causal
model determines which warrant intervention (the top correlational hub BUSD proved non-pivotal), so
treat the GAT score as a triage signal, not a direct intervention target.

\section{Conclusion}
On real multi-venue data, day-scale stablecoin contagion is predictable while hour-scale onset is
not, and graph attention adds real, \emph{edge-borne} signal. Across five seeds on the held-out SVB
cluster GAT reaches PR-AUC $0.447\pm0.016$, beating XGBoost by $+0.175$ on every seed, and a
four-condition ablation pins $+0.10$ of that margin to the directed lead-lag \emph{edges} rather than
the neural architecture or feature domain (on-chain proxy features add only $+0.005$ to XGBoost).
Attention names the mechanism: the USDC\,$\to$\,TUSD channel carries $1.9\times$ average attention
across crisis episodes, and explicit temporal memory adds little ($+0.008$), consistent with the 6h
rolling graph already encoding the dynamics. XGBoost's top feature is the OU mean-reversion
half-life (gain~$88$ vs realised-volatility $65$), confirming that peg-recovery speed drives the
early-warning signal more than volatility level. Four caveats bound the claim. The most important is that
the cross-crisis advantage is \emph{conditional}: GAT's mean leave-one-cluster-out margin is positive
on all seven episodes but reverses on the stable-five subset (\S\ref{sec:results}); we report this
boundary rather than an unconditional win (the held-out SVB margin of $+0.175$ stays robust across
seeds). The other three are data and deployment limits: seven episodes on two exchanges (two too
sparse); lead-lag edges can over-credit a thinly traded coin that only \emph{appears} to lead; and
raw outputs need isotonic recalibration (GAT ECE $0.26\to0.02$, PR-AUC preserved by monotonicity). The most consequential caveat is that the ranking is
\emph{correlational}: a venue can top it while being irrelevant to containment, which is why handing
it to an explicit causal test is the responsible deployment and motivates the companion study (anonymized). All
results reproduce from a single committed script.
